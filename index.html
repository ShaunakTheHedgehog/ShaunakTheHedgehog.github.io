About

I am a second-year PhD student in the Princeton Neuroscience Institute (PNI) at Princeton University, where I am fortunate to be co-advised by Dr. Jonathan Pillow and Dr. Boris Hanin. I am currently supported through the Quantitative and Computational Neuroscience Training Program (QNTP) at Princeton.

My research sits at the intersection of computational neuroscience and machine learning theory, with a particular focus on understanding the mechanisms governing learning in both biological and artificial neural systems. On the neuroscience side, I am interested in inferring what learning algorithms real animals use to learn new tasks. On the machine learning theory side, I hope to explain how and in what settings such learning algorithms can produce successful learning.

Previously, I completed my undergraduate degree in Mathematics and Symbolic Systems at Stanford University. Before coming to Princeton, I worked with Dr. James McClelland to build biologically plausible neural network models of memory, inspired by the complex computations performed by contemporary memory- and attention-based AI systems. I also worked with Dr. Scott Linderman to understand how neuromodulatory mechanisms can shape flexible neural computation in recurrent neural networks.
